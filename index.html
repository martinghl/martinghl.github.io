About me

My name is Guanghao (Martin) Li, and I’m originally from Beijing, China. Moving to the U.S. at 15 sparked my curiosity and taught me how to navigate unfamiliar environments, something I’ve grown to appreciate throughout my academic journey. I’m currently pursuing a Master’s degree in Biostatistics at NYU, where my research focuses on gene regulatory networks and machine learning in public health.

Outside of academics, I enjoy hiking, practicing archery, and mentoring students in R programming as a Teaching Assistant. I’m committed to using data-driven approaches to make meaningful contributions to health research.

    My First Blog

    As I sit down to write this, I realize I’m in the middle of one of the most pivotal transitions of my life—applying for PhD programs. It’s a process that feels both exciting and daunting, but more than anything, it’s a reminder of how far I’ve come and how much I’ve learned along the way. Looking back, the journey feels like a series of leaps—some calculated, others taken on faith. Moving to the U.S. at 15 with barely any English skills was probably the first of these leaps. I remember feeling lost but determined. Those early days taught me resilience, something I’ve leaned on time and again throughout my academic and professional life. In many ways, my path to biomedical informatics and biostatistics has been a winding one. I started with a solid foundation in statistics, but my interests evolved as I explored new challenges. Working on cervical cancer classification, I discovered the power of machine learning to tackle real-world problems. Building predictive models for cervical cancer diagnosis taught me the importance of addressing bias and class imbalances in datasets—insights that are now second nature in my approach to research. When I joined Huang Lab, I didn’t have a background in biology. I remember feeling like an outsider at first, surrounded by peers who seemed to know everything about gene regulatory networks. But that didn’t stop me. I immersed myself in the field, balancing a crash course in biology with the coding and data analysis that I already knew how to do. That experience didn’t just teach me about gene networks—it showed me how much I enjoy diving into the unknown and figuring things out. This year has been transformative. My thesis focuses on comparing gene regulatory networks in Ulcerative Colitis and Healthy Controls. It’s a project that combines everything I love—statistical rigor, network analysis, and the pursuit of understanding how biology intersects with disease. At the same time, I’ve been working on analyzing data from a longitudinal phone survey in Kenya, uncovering factors behind participant retention and loss to follow-up. These projects have given me a sense of purpose and direction, not to mention the confidence to take on new challenges. It hasn’t been all work, though. Being a Teaching Assistant at NYU has been one of the most rewarding aspects of my journey. Helping students navigate the nuances of R programming and statistical methods reminds me why I love learning in the first place. It’s a privilege to help others find their footing, just as I once struggled to find mine. As I submit my applications to PhD programs, I can’t help but feel a mix of emotions—hope, nervousness, and a deep sense of gratitude. This journey has been long and, at times, uncertain, but it’s also been filled with growth and discovery. Whatever happens next, I’m ready to take the leap. Here’s to the next chapter—whatever it may hold.
